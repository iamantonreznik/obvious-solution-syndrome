# Obvious Solution Syndrome
## What It Is
The tendency to take the first plausible-sounding solution to a complex problem and accept it as the answer, without actually understanding the problem itself.

Most often, such a solution sounds convincing and fits the format "if X, do Y."
It creates a feeling that the issue is resolved, the problem is less severe, or that the solution is actually simple. This feeling builds a habit of thinking this way and reinforces the syndrome.

Decisions made under this syndrome are not necessarily wrong. Often they even work, or at least partially work.
The problem with the syndrome is not the solution or how quickly you get it. The problem is that understanding the situation was replaced by delivering an answer.
## How to Recognize It
- A complex question gets a short and confident answer
- The answer sounds like a ready-made formula: "just do this," "just use X"
- No analysis of what is actually going on - instead, an answer of what to do
- When challenged with "but what if...", the answer either falls apart or is defended with yet another ready-made formula
## How It Forms
The syndrome is shaped by events or biases, usually working together:
- Cognitive economy. Thinking is expensive; a ready answer is cheap.
- Social pressure. Not having an answer means looking incompetent, defeated, inexperienced, out of control, or weak (in front of reports, peers).
- Pattern illusion. The situation looks like a familiar one, so the brain pulls an answer from memory without checking how similar it really is.
- Emotional reaction. A tense situation, a problem case, a streak of difficulties, or any other negative state demands fixing - often right here, right now.
- AI and the culture of instant answers. The habit of getting instant answers, or thinking through AI, removes the need to pause for your own analysis and builds a mindset of expectations.
## Why It Is Harmful
- Creates the illusion of a solution. It feels like the issue is resolved, when in reality it has only been postponed.
- Stops thinking. Having given an answer, the brain stops working on the problem, and the real complexity remains unexamined.
- Reproduces itself. If an obvious solution "worked" (or seemed to work), next time the person applies it even faster, thinking even less.
- Passed on to others as advice. The source of the obvious solution often bears no responsibility and does not suffer from the consequences.
## Where It Shows Up
- In people management
- In technical decisions with long-term consequences
- In learning and development
- In AI responses to complex questions
- In life situations and decision-making
## Examples
### Management
> - An employee is demotivated and performing poorly.
> - Have a one-on-one, find out what's wrong. If that doesn't help - put them on a PIP, set clear goals.

Sounds like a targeted instruction backed by expertise. Yet none of the real questions are asked: why do you think they are demotivated? Maybe they are overloaded, burned out, unclear on priorities, disagree with the direction, or mentally checked out. Each of these cases requires a different response. "Have a one-on-one" only defines the format. It does not become an action - anything can happen inside that format.
### Technical Decisions
> - The database is slow.
> - Add indexes. Spin up a read replica.

Sounds technically correct. Not examined: what exactly is slow, on which queries, at what times, under what load, what changed. An index can speed up one query and slow down others. A replica may not help if the bottleneck is writes or locks.
### Personal Life
> - I'm tired at work.
> - Take a vacation.

A universal obvious solution that often works. Yet sometimes a vacation changes nothing, because the fatigue comes from the nature of the work, relationships, or loss of meaning - not from volume. The obvious solution masks the real cause.
### Education and Learning
> - I don't understand the topic.
> - Read book X / watch course Y.

Often useless. The point of confusion is unclear, it is unclear what exactly is missing - facts, context, application experience, a mental model. The book/course satisfies the feeling of "I'm doing something," but not the point where understanding got stuck.
## What Does Not Solve It
- Artificial pauses. "Let's be quiet and think" without understanding what information is needed and where to get it. Doesn't engage thinking - just delays it.
- Consulting on every question. Pulling people into discussions instead of independent analysis. Group analysis is for complex situations, not for when you're afraid to decide alone.
- Ignoring past decisions. Rejecting others' or your own experience out of a desire to do it your way. A healthy approach includes asking "who has already dealt with this."
- The loudest voice. Accepting a decision because the most senior person said it. This changes the form of the syndrome but does not escape it - there is still no analysis of the situation.
## How to Solve It (tl;dr)
Stop rushing. Gather information. Look at alternative solutions. Acknowledge uncertainty. Don't give up on making a decision, but don't try to always solve it right now. Know about the XY problem.
## How to Solve It
The syndrome is broken down by rethinking the system of decision-making and responding to difficulties.

A simplified model for understanding a decision looks like this:
```
problem
  |
  v
analysis
  |
  v
choosing a solution
  |
  v
making the decision
  ├── expected consequences
  └── unexpected consequences
```
This model lets you acknowledge that every decision has a lifecycle - part of it ends for a person in a predictable way, and part in an unpredictable way. Understanding this model helps reduce unexpected consequences and manage the situation/problem better.

This makes it clear that analyzing the situation before making a decision becomes the defining factor for overcoming the Obvious Solution Syndrome. Analysis helps:
- gather information about the situation/problem from multiple perspectives and see elements that are not on the surface
- formulate decision forks and look at the cost of their consequences ("But what if...")
- acknowledge uncertainty where it exists ("I don't know, I need to look into it" is not a bad answer)
- let go of the desire to solve the problem right now - not every problem requires immediate action
- be confident that we are solving the right problem (the XY problem)
